# PawPal+ Behavior and Training Guidelines

## Basic Training Principles

### Positive Reinforcement
- **Definition**: Rewarding desired behavior to increase its frequency
- **Rewards**: Treats, praise, playtime, or toys
- **Timing**: Reward within 1-2 seconds of desired behavior for clarity
- **Consistency**: Same family members should use same cues and rewards
- **Effectiveness**: Most humane and effective training method

### Punishment Avoidance
- **Why avoid**: Creates fear, damages trust, can increase aggression
- **Alternatives**: Redirect unwanted behavior, remove reward, ignore behavior
- **Never use**: Physical punishment, yelling, harsh corrections
- **Professional help**: Use trainer if behavior escalates

### Training Session Guidelines
- **Duration**: 5-15 minutes for dogs, 2-5 minutes for cats
- **Frequency**: 2-3 sessions daily for best results
- **Timing**: Before meals or normal playtime to increase motivation
- **Environment**: Start in quiet environment, gradually add distractions
- **Patience**: Most behaviors take 2-4 weeks to establish

## Dogs

### Housebreaking (Puppies)
- **Age to start**: 12-16 weeks when bladder control develops
- **Frequency**: Take out first thing in morning, after meals, after play, before bed
- **Cues**: Use consistent phrase like "go potty"
- **Accidents**: Never punish; clean with enzymatic cleaner to remove scent
- **Success rate**: Most puppies can be reliably housebroken by 4-6 months

### Basic Commands (Sit, Stay, Come)
- **Sit**: Lure with treat above nose, mark with "yes" when rear touches ground, reward
- **Stay**: Start with 2-3 seconds, gradually increase duration
- **Come**: Start with short distances, use excited voice, high-value rewards
- **Repetition**: Practice daily in short sessions
- **Distractions**: Master in quiet area before adding people or other dogs

### Leash Training
- **Equipment**: Use properly fitted collar or harness, 4-6 foot leash
- **Initial walks**: Keep short (10-15 minutes), allow exploration
- **Pulling**: Stop walking when dog pulls, resume when leash is slack
- **Duration**: Adult dogs need 30-60 minutes daily exercise
- **Socialization**: Expose to different environments, sounds, people

### Destructive Behavior
- **Causes**: Boredom, separation anxiety, teething, lack of exercise
- **Prevention**: Adequate exercise, mental stimulation, appropriate toys
- **Management**: Crate training, removing access to household items
- **Exercise needs**: 1-2 hours daily for most adult dogs
- **Toys**: Rotate toys, provide puzzle toys and chew toys

### Jumping and Greeting
- **Root cause**: Excitement, seeking attention
- **Solution**: Turn away when jumping, reward when all four paws on ground
- **Consistency**: All people must follow same rules
- **Greeting protocol**: Practice before guests arrive
- **Timeline**: May take 4-6 weeks with consistent practice

### Socialization
- **Window**: 3-14 weeks is critical period
- **Exposure**: Different people, animals, sounds, environments
- **Positive outcomes**: Pair new experiences with treats and praise
- **Fear periods**: Don't force; let dog investigate at own pace
- **Lack of socialization**: Can lead to fear-based aggression later

## Cats

### Litter Box Training
- **Setup**: Litter box placement away from food/water and sleeping areas
- **Cleanliness**: Scoop daily, full litter change weekly
- **Box size**: Should be 1.5x length of cat
- **Number of boxes**: Minimum one per cat plus one extra
- **Accidents**: Check for medical issues, stress, or litter box aversion
- **Substrate**: Experiment if cat avoids box (clay, crystal, paper, pine)

### Scratching Posts and Furniture Protection
- **Scratching need**: Natural, essential behavior for cats
- **Posts**: 3+ feet tall, sturdy, covered in rope or sisal
- **Placement**: Near sleeping areas, by windows, high-traffic areas
- **Encouragement**: Sprinkle catnip, use play to draw attention to post
- **Furniture**: Use deterrent sprays, double-sided tape, or furniture covers
- **Declawing**: Not recommended; equivalent to removing fingertips

### Handling and Touch Tolerance
- **Early socialization**: Start young for best results
- **Short sessions**: Brief touching to build tolerance
- **Respect boundaries**: Watch for ear flattening, tail swishing (signs of stress)
- **Positive association**: Pair handling with treats and gentle voice
- **Professional help**: Groomer or vet for nail trimming if cat won't tolerate

### Aggression and Redirected Aggression
- **Types**: Fear aggression, resource guarding, play aggression, redirected
- **Prevention**: Regular play with appropriate toys, environmental enrichment
- **Warning signs**: Hissing, swatting, pinning ears, tense body
- **Management**: Separate cats during playtime, provide separate resources
- **Professional help**: Consult veterinary behaviorist for severe cases

### Environmental Enrichment
- **Window perches**: Cats enjoy watching outside activity
- **Vertical spaces**: Cat trees, shelves for climbing
- **Play schedule**: 10-15 minutes of interactive play 2-3 times daily
- **Toys rotation**: Change toys every few weeks to maintain interest
- **Mental stimulation**: Puzzle feeders, hide treats around house

## Common Behavioral Issues

### Separation Anxiety
- **Signs**: Excessive barking, destructive behavior, elimination when alone
- **Prevention**: Gradual alone time exposure, crate training
- **Management**: Start with leaving for short periods, return calmly
- **Treatment**: May require medication and professional trainer
- **Exercise**: Tire out pet before departure with play or walk

### Fear and Phobias
- **Common triggers**: Thunder, fireworks, vacuum, car travel
- **Management**: Provide safe space (room, crate, closet), white noise
- **Desensitization**: Gradual exposure with positive associations
- **Medication**: Anxiety medication available from veterinarian
- **Do not force**: Forcing interaction with feared object increases anxiety

### Resource Guarding
- **Signs**: Stiffening, growling, eating faster around people or other pets
- **Prevention**: Hand-feeding, regular feeding schedule, separate feeding areas
- **Training**: May require professional trainer
- **Safety**: Separate pets during feeding time
- **Severity**: Severe cases may require behavioral medication

## Professional Training

### When to Seek Help
- **Aggression**: Any biting or severe aggression
- **Fear-based issues**: Not improving with home training
- **Behavioral regression**: Sudden changes in behavior
- **Safety concerns**: Potential danger to self, people, or other animals

### Types of Trainers
- **Certified trainers**: Certified Professional Dog Trainers (CPDT)
- **Behaviorists**: Board-certified veterinary behaviorists (ACVB)
- **Avoid**: Trainers using punishment or aversive methods
- **Group classes**: Good for puppy socialization and basic obedience
- **Private training**: Better for behavioral issues
