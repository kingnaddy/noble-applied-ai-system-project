# PawPal+ Health and Safety Guidelines

## ⚠️ Emergency Warning

**This information is educational only and is NOT a substitute for professional veterinary care. For medical emergencies, immediately contact your veterinarian or emergency animal hospital.**

## Preventive Care Schedule

### Puppies (0-1 year)
- **Vaccinations**: At 6-8, 10-12, 14-16 weeks, then annual booster
- **Deworming**: At 2, 4, 6, 8 weeks, then monthly until 6 months
- **Flea/Tick prevention**: Start at 6-8 weeks, year-round
- **Spay/neuter**: Typically at 5-6 months
- **First vet visit**: Within 48 hours of adoption

### Adult Dogs (1-7 years)
- **Vaccinations**: Annual booster after initial series
- **Flea/Tick prevention**: Year-round
- **Heartworm testing**: Annually before starting/continuing prevention
- **Dental check-ups**: Annually, professional cleaning as needed
- **Physical exam**: Annually, twice yearly for geriatric dogs

### Senior Dogs (7+ years)
- **Vet visits**: Twice yearly (every 6 months)
- **Blood work**: Annual baseline, more frequent as issues arise
- **Weight monitoring**: Check monthly as metabolism changes
- **Vaccinations**: Discuss frequency with vet based on health
- **Dental care**: More frequent professional cleanings may be needed

### Puppies and Kittens (0-1 year)
- **Vaccinations**: At 6-9, 12-16 weeks, then annual booster
- **Deworming**: At 2-4, 6-8, 10-12 weeks, then monthly until 6 months
- **Flea/Tick prevention**: Start at 8 weeks, year-round
- **Spay/neuter**: Typically at 5-6 months
- **First vet visit**: Within 48 hours of adoption

### Adult Cats (1-7 years)
- **Vaccinations**: Annual booster after initial series
- **Flea/Tick prevention**: Year-round, even for indoor cats
- **Dental check-ups**: Annually
- **Physical exam**: Annually
- **Weight monitoring**: Monthly, cats prone to obesity

### Senior Cats (10+ years)
- **Vet visits**: Twice yearly (every 6 months)
- **Blood work**: Annual baseline, more frequent with health issues
- **Kidney function**: Monitor closely, common in older cats
- **Dental care**: Increased risk of tooth loss and disease
- **Diet adjustments**: May need kidney-friendly or senior formula food

## Common Health Conditions

### Obesity
- **Signs**: Inability to feel ribs, waist not visible from above
- **Prevention**: Portion control, regular exercise, healthy treats
- **Treatment**: Gradual calorie reduction, increased activity, vet-supervised diet
- **Risks**: Diabetes, joint problems, shortened lifespan
- **Monitoring**: Weigh monthly, adjust portions based on trend

### Diabetes Mellitus
- **Signs in dogs**: Increased thirst, frequent urination, weight loss, increased appetite
- **Signs in cats**: Increased thirst, increased urination, weight loss, lethargy
- **Management**: Insulin injections (usually twice daily), diet management
- **Feeding**: Give insulin right before meals, follow strict schedule
- **Monitoring**: Regular blood glucose checks with veterinarian
- **Outcome**: Many cats can go into remission with proper management

### Urinary Tract Infections (UTIs)
- **Signs**: Frequent urination, straining, blood in urine, accidents
- **Prevention**: Adequate hydration, clean litter boxes, regular emptying
- **Treatment**: Antibiotics prescribed by veterinarian
- **Testing**: Urinalysis required for diagnosis
- **Prevention in cats**: Ensure multiple water sources, wet food

### Dental Disease
- **Signs**: Bad breath, yellow/brown teeth, drooling, difficulty eating
- **Prevention**: Daily brushing, annual professional cleaning, hard toys
- **Progression**: Leads to tooth loss, bone loss, organ damage
- **Risk factors**: Small breed dogs, older pets, plaque buildup
- **Treatment**: Professional cleaning under anesthesia when needed

### Arthritis
- **Signs**: Limping, stiffness, difficulty getting up, avoiding stairs
- **Common in**: Large breed dogs, older cats, obese pets
- **Management**: Regular exercise, weight management, pain medication
- **Supplements**: Glucosamine, chondroitin, omega-3 may help
- **Environmental**: Ramps, orthopedic beds, easy access to resources

## Toxins and Poisons

### Foods Toxic to Dogs
- **Chocolate**: Contains theobromine, dark chocolate most dangerous
- **Grapes and raisins**: Can cause kidney failure
- **Onions and garlic**: Damage red blood cells
- **Avocado**: Contains persin toxin
- **Xylitol**: Artificial sweetener in sugar-free foods, causes hypoglycemia
- **Alcohol**: Even small amounts dangerous
- **Macadamia nuts**: Cause weakness and tremors
- **Action**: If ingested, contact veterinarian or poison control immediately

### Foods Toxic to Cats
- **Onions and garlic**: More sensitive than dogs
- **Chocolate**: Toxic, especially dark chocolate
- **Lilies**: Extremely toxic, all parts (including pollen)
- **Raw dough**: Can expand in stomach
- **Raw eggs**: Risk of salmonella and E. coli
- **Raw fish**: Can destroy thiamine (Vitamin B1)
- **Grapes and raisins**: Can cause kidney failure
- **Action**: If ingested, contact veterinarian immediately

### Household Toxins
- **Medications**: Keep all pills in secure containers
- **Cleaning supplies**: Store in locked cabinet
- **Pesticides**: Use pet-safe options
- **Plant toxins**: Remove toxic plants from home
- **Antifreeze**: Even small amounts are deadly (smell sweet)
- **Lead**: Old paint, some toys contain lead

### Symptom Checklist
- Vomiting or diarrhea
- Drooling or difficulty swallowing
- Lethargy or weakness
- Seizures or tremors
- Rapid breathing
- Collapse

**Action**: Contact veterinarian or animal poison control immediately

## Parasite Prevention

### External Parasites (Fleas and Ticks)
- **Prevention**: Monthly topical, oral, or collar treatment
- **Year-round**: Essential even in winter climates
- **Tick removal**: Use tweezers to grasp near head, pull straight out
- **Disease transmission**: Fleas transmit tapeworms; ticks carry Lyme disease
- **Environment**: Regular vacuuming, washing bedding in hot water

### Internal Parasites (Worms)
- **Types**: Roundworms, hookworms, tapeworms, whipworms
- **Prevention**: Regular deworming schedule for puppies/kittens
- **Adult prevention**: Flea prevention also controls some worms
- **Testing**: Annual fecal exam by veterinarian
- **Transmission**: Some parasites transmissible to humans (zoonotic)

### Heartworms
- **Prevention**: Monthly preventive medication required
- **Testing**: Annual heartworm test before starting prevention
- **Symptom**: Cough, lethargy, difficulty breathing (advanced)
- **Risk**: Fatal if untreated, common in warm climates
- **Treatment**: Expensive, requires hospitalization, can be dangerous

## First Aid and Injury Management

### Cuts and Wounds
- **Minor**: Clean with mild soap and water, apply antibiotic ointment
- **Bleeding**: Apply pressure with clean cloth for 5-10 minutes
- **Deep wounds**: Seek veterinary care, may need stitches
- **Infection signs**: Increased swelling, discharge, odor
- **Elizabethan collar**: May be needed to prevent licking

### Sprains and Limping
- **Acute injury**: Rest, ice for 15-20 minutes, limit activity
- **Limping duration**: If limping >24 hours, see veterinarian
- **Analgesics**: Over-the-counter pain relief not recommended for pets
- **Recovery**: Usually 2-3 weeks with restricted activity
- **Physical therapy**: May be recommended for recovery

### Burns and Heat-Related Issues
- **Heat stroke signs**: Excessive panting, drooling, weakness, collapse
- **Action**: Move to cool area, apply cool (not ice) water to body
- **Hospitalization**: Often needed for heat stroke
- **Prevention**: Never leave pet in car, provide water and shade
- **Burns**: Cool with water, cover with clean cloth, seek vet care

## Medication Administration

### Pill Administration
- **Timing**: Consistent time daily, usually with food unless instructed otherwise
- **Method**: Hide in treat (pill pocket), use pill crusher if approved, or place directly in mouth
- **Checking**: Verify pill went down, not spit out
- **Duration**: Complete full course even if symptoms improve
- **Storage**: Keep in original bottle, out of reach

### Injection Administration
- **Training**: Veterinarian should demonstrate technique first
- **Sterile technique**: Use clean needle, don't reuse
- **Rotation**: Alternate injection sites to prevent soreness
- **Timing**: Consistent time daily if possible
- **Monitoring**: Watch injection site for swelling or infection

### Eye/Ear Drops
- **Positioning**: Have helper hold pet calm
- **Cleanliness**: Wash hands before application
- **Drops**: Hold dropper at appropriate distance, avoid touching eye/ear
- **Duration**: Follow completion instructions carefully
- **Repeat**: Follow frequency exactly as prescribed

## Reproduction and Spaying/Neutering

### Spaying (Ovariohysterectomy)
- **Timing**: Typically at 5-6 months before first heat
- **Benefits**: Prevents uterine infections, breast cancer, unwanted pregnancy
- **Estrus cycle**: Heat cycles stop after spaying
- **Recovery**: 10-14 days, limit activity during healing
- **Cost**: Varies by location, typically $200-$500

### Neutering (Castration)
- **Timing**: Typically at 5-6 months
- **Benefits**: Prevents testicular cancer, reduces aggression/roaming
- **Behavioral changes**: May reduce unwanted behaviors
- **Recovery**: 7-10 days, limit activity during healing
- **Cost**: Varies by location, typically $200-$500

### Intact Pets
- **Heat cycles (dogs)**: Every 6-12 months, bleeding for 5-14 days
- **Heat cycles (cats)**: Every 1-2 weeks during breeding season
- **Marking behavior**: Males mark more frequently (spraying in cats)
- **Unwanted behaviors**: Can include aggression, roaming, mounting
- **Overpopulation**: Contributes to shelter overcrowding and euthanasia
